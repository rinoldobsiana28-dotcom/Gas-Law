Understanding the Laws of Gases

The behaviour of an ideal gas is elegantly summarised by the ideal gas law (PV = nRT), which unifies the individual contributions of Boyle, Charles, Gay‑Lussac, and Avogadro. Below you will find interactive simulations that let you observe each relationship in real time, along with dedicated calculators to solve quantitative problems. Boyle's Law

At constant temperature, the pressure of a fixed amount of gas is inversely proportional to its volume. According to Boyle’s law (P₁V₁ = P₂V₂), moving the piston to reduce the volume causes the particles to hit the walls more frequently, raising the pressure. This foundational principle is also explained by NASA’s Glenn Research Center.

Charles's Law

At constant pressure, the volume of a given mass of gas is directly proportional to its absolute temperature, as described by Charles’s law (V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂). Raise the temperature slider and watch the balloon expand – this is why hot air balloons rise. NASA’s beginner guide provides further context.

⚠️ Temperature must be in Kelvin (add 273.15 to °C)

Gay‑Lussac's Law

At constant volume, the pressure of a gas is directly proportional to its absolute temperature, as stated by Gay‑Lussac’s law (P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂). Higher temperature means faster particles and more forceful wall collisions. This relationship is critical in pressure cooker design, as explained by NASA’s education page.

Avogadro's Law

At constant temperature and pressure, the volume of a gas is directly proportional to the number of moles, as formalised by Avogadro’s law (V₁/n₁ = V₂/n₂). Click the button to “pump” particles into the container and observe the wall expansion. For a broader kinetic‑molecular perspective, visit the Khan Academy gas behaviour unit.
